To move into high-level Software Engineering and Backend roles at companies like Google or Microsoft without following the standard "Full Stack" path, you need channels that focus on **Systems, Scalability, and Clean Architecture**.

Here are the best resources to help you transition your C++ logic into Java-based industry projects and internship strategies.

### 1. Java & Backend Mastery (The "How to Build" Phase)

These channels focus on the industry-standard way to write Java (using Spring Boot, Microservices, and Docker).

* **Amigoscode:** This is the gold standard for "Production-Ready" code. He covers Java, Spring Boot, and how to use databases like PostgreSQL. He focuses on the "Professional" way to structure a project, not just getting it to work.
* **Java Brains (Koushik Kothagal):** Perfect for understanding the deep internals of Java and Enterprise systems. If you want to know *why* a certain design pattern is used in a massive backend, this is the place.
* **Tech Primers:** This channel is excellent for understanding **Microservices, Kafka, and Redis**. These are the tools that make a backend "Advanced" rather than just "Basic."
* **Dan Vega:** If you want to see exactly how to integrate AI into a Java application, check his **"Spring AI"** tutorials. He shows how to bridge the gap between Java backends and Large Language Models (LLMs).

### 2. Systems & High-Level Design (The "FAANG" Bar)

To get into Big Tech, you need to prove you understand how large systems work. These channels are more technical and less "tutorial" based.

* **Arpit Bhayani:** An ex-Google/Amazon engineer who does deep dives into how systems like Redis or WhatsApp are built. He discusses high-level architecture and system internals. This is "Software Engineering" in its purest form.
* **Gaurav Sen:** Excellent for **System Design**. He breaks down complex problems (like Horizontal Scaling and Load Balancing) in a way that is essential for 3rd and 4th-year internship interviews.
* **ByteByteGo:** This channel uses visual diagrams to explain how massive companies handle millions of requests. It’s perfect for learning how to design a "Reconciliation Engine" or a "High-Frequency Trading" backend.

### 3. Career, Internships, and Strategy

For the "shit" part (how to actually get the internship and navigate the Indian tier-2 landscape):

* **Sanket Singh (Algocamp):** As someone who has worked at Google, Meta, and Microsoft, he gives very specific advice on what these companies look for in students from tier-2 colleges. He often talks about why "Full Stack" isn't the only way.
* **Kunal Kushwaha:** A strong advocate for **Open Source**. If you want to get noticed by Big Tech without a "Full Stack" resume, contributing to massive Java-based open-source projects (like those under the Apache Foundation) is a proven path.
* **The Primeagen:** This is for the "Engineering Mindset." He focuses on low-level efficiency, the "why" behind technologies, and career advice that cuts through the marketing fluff. It’s great for developing a "Vim-using, performance-obsessed" engineering persona.

### Project Idea for your 3rd Semester

Since you want to focus on Software Engineering + AI in the Finance domain, here is a project that would stand out to a Google recruiter more than any website:

**The Project: "Predictive High-Throughput Transaction Validator"**

1. **Backend (Java):** Build a system that ingests 10,000 "dummy" financial transactions per second using **Java Threads**.
2. **The Engine:** Use a **Trie** or **Inverted Index** (your DSA skills) to instantly check for duplicate or fraudulent transactions.
3. **The AI Layer:** Use a small Java-based ML library (like **Deep Netts** or **Weka**) to predict which transactions are likely to be "flagged" based on past data.
4. **The Moat:** Document why you chose Java's **Virtual Threads** for concurrency and how you optimized the search using specific Data Structures.
